Featured Project

PlateLensAI

An AI-powered mobile application that allows Nigerian university students to photograph their meals and receive instant, personalised nutritional feedback — built from scratch, shipped end-to-end, and designed for the Nigerian food context that every global nutrition app ignores.

AI / Machine Learning Product Strategy User Research PRD Mobile — React Native MobileNetV2 Python Figma

The Problem

Nobody was
building for Temi.

01

No Nigerian nutrition app existed

Over 2 million Nigerian university students had zero access to a nutrition tool that recognised their actual meals — jollof rice, egusi soup, suya, amala.

02

Western apps were completely irrelevant

MyFitnessPal and similar products have no Nigerian food database. Students who tried them gave up within days. The product was not built for them.

03

Nutrition felt complex and academic

Existing resources — textbooks, NAFDAC guidelines — were inaccessible to the average student. Nobody had made nutritional guidance feel simple, personal, and instant.

Research

What the users
actually said.

Finding 01 — Awareness Gap
"I eat the same 3 things every day. I don't know if that's good or bad." — 8 of 12 students interviewed reported eating the same limited set of meals repeatedly with no nutritional awareness.
Finding 02 — Tool Abandonment
90% of existing nutrition apps tested during research had no Nigerian food entries. Students who tried them abandoned within 3 days — not from laziness, but from irrelevance.
Finding 03 — Friction is the enemy
Manual food logging was rejected universally. "I'm not going to type everything I eat." The only acceptable interaction model was: take a photo, get an answer.
Insight that shaped the product
The product had to be Nigerian-first, effortless, and non-judgmental. One photo. Instant result. No guilt. No complexity. That became the product's north star.

PRD Snapshot

Objective
Give Nigerian university students instant, accurate nutritional feedback on the meals they actually eat — making balanced nutrition accessible without expertise.
Target Users
Nigerian university students aged 18–28, eating campus canteen or home-cooked meals, heavy smartphone users with limited nutritional knowledge.
Core Requirements
Meal photo scan with AI classification · Nutrition feedback report · Food group dashboard · Meal history · Push notification reminders.
Non-Goals (v1)
Calorie counting · Meal planning · Social features · Web version · Monetisation.
North Star Metric
Weekly Active Meal Scans — users returning to scan at least 3x per week.

Reflection

What I learned.
What I would change.

What I Learned

Owning an AI product end-to-end taught me that the hardest PM skill is not writing the PRD — it is deciding what not to build. Every feature cut was a negotiation between user delight and shipping reality.

Data quality is a product problem, not just an engineering problem. The model was only as good as the Nigerian food images we trained it on — and curating that dataset required as much product thinking as any feature decision.

User interviews conducted in the early research phase saved weeks of building the wrong thing. The insight that manual logging was universally rejected changed the entire product direction before a single line of code was written.

What I Would Improve

I would run a structured beta test with 30–50 real university students before calling v1 complete. Shipping to real users earlier — even with a rougher product — would have generated better model correction data and stronger retention insights.

The onboarding flow could be reduced from 4 screens to 2. In retrospect, collecting dietary restrictions upfront added friction without meaningfully improving the first-time experience.

I would define success metrics more rigorously before building, not after. The North Star Metric — Weekly Active Meal Scans — should have been the compass for every feature decision from day one.
