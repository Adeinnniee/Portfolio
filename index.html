Featured Project

PlateLensAI

Most nutrition apps were not built with Nigerian students in mind. PlateLensAI is a mobile application that uses AI to identify the food groups in a meal, simply from a picture, and deliver instant, personalised nutritional feedback. Built around locally eaten foods like jollof rice, egusi soup, suya, and ogi, it brings nutritional awareness to the dietary realities most students actually live with.

AI / Machine Learning Product Strategy User Research PRD Mobile React Native MobileNetV2 Python Figma

The Problem

Nobody was
building for Temi.

01

Built for the meals Nigerian students actually eat

Many Nigerian university students lacked a nutrition tool that truly recognized everyday local meals like jollof rice, egusi soup, suya, and amala.

02

Western apps didn't reflect local eating habits

MyFitnessPal and similar apps have limited to no Nigerian food data. Students who tried them gave up quickly, the products simply weren't built for what they eat daily.

03

Nutrition information was hard to access and harder to act on

Existing resources like textbooks and NAFDAC guidelines were written for professionals, not students. No one had made nutritional guidance feel simple, personal, or immediate.

Research

What the users
actually said.

Finding 01 - Awareness Gap
"I eat the same 3 things every day. I don't know if that's good or bad." 8 out of 12 students interviewed reported eating the same limited set of meals repeatedly with no nutritional awareness.
Finding 02 - Tool Abandonment
During research, 6 out of 7 nutrition apps tested had no Nigerian food entries. Students who tried them stopped within days, not from laziness, but because the tools weren't built for what they actually eat.
Finding 03 - Friction is the enemy
Manual food logging was rejected universally. "I'm not going to type everything I eat." The only acceptable interaction model was: take a picture, get an answer.
Finding 04 - The snack problem
Students often upload snack pictures like biscuits, crisps, and chin-chin. Returning an error or empty result would feel broken and dismissive, so a 7th class, Snacks, was added to handle them gracefully.
Insight that shaped the product
The product had to be Nigerian-first, effortless, and non-judgmental. One picture. Instant result. No guilt. No complexity. That became the product's north star.

PRD Snapshot

Objective
Give Nigerian university students instant, personalised nutritional feedback on the meals they actually eat; through an AI model trained on local foods and validated by nutrition professionals.
Target Users
Nigerian university students aged 18-28, eating campus canteen or home-cooked meals, heavy smartphone users with limited nutritional knowledge.
Core Requirements
Meal picture capture or upload with AI food detection and classification · Nutritionist-validated food knowledge base · Nutrition feedback report · Personalised tips for achieving a balanced plate · Meal history · App Preferences and Push notification reminders.
Non - Goals (v1)
Calorie counting · Meal planning · Social features · Web version · Monetisation.
North Star Metric
Weekly Active Meal Analyses: users returning to analyse at least 3 meals per week.

Reflection

What I learned.
What I would change.

What I Learned

Owning an AI product end-to-end taught me that the hardest PM skill is not writing the PRD, it is deciding what not to build. Every feature cut was a negotiation between user delight and shipping reality.

Data quality is a product problem, not just an engineering one. The model was only as good as the Nigerian food images we trained it on, and curating that dataset required as much product thinking as any feature decision.

User interviews in the early research phase saved weeks of building the wrong thing. The insight that manual food logging was universally rejected changed the entire product direction before a single line of code was written.

Adding Snacks was a deliberate product decision. Nigerian students treat snacks as part of their daily meals, and naming them directly helped preserve trust while keeping the experience honest.

What I Would Improve

I would run a structured beta test with 30–50 real university students before calling v1 complete. Shipping to real users earlier, even with a rougher product, would have generated better model correction data and stronger retention insights.

I would define success metrics more rigorously before building, not after. The north star metric, Weekly Active Meal Analyses, should have been the compass for every feature decision from day one.

The model achieved 67% validation accuracy on a 300-image Nigerian food dataset. Expanding the training data is the clearest path to improving this, both the quality and volume of images directly determine model performance.

I would prioritize labeling identified food groups directly on the meal image. Due to technical constraints, only the analysis summary and tips were implemented. Adding the full visual flow later would make the AI feedback feel more grounded and intuitive.
